    Introduction

    Neurons communicate with each other using electrical impulses that carry specific information for various activities. The electroencephalogram (EEG) serves as the conventional method for recording neural signals, encompassing diverse waves and spikes characterized by varying frequencies and amplitudes. These signals are captured through the placement of a predetermined set of electrodes on the human scalp. The intricate neural network is formed by the collaboration of billions of neurons transmitting electrical impulses.

    The recording of EEG signals presents several challenges. Beyond cerebral activity, there are additional sources that generate undesired electrical interferences, which become mixed with cerebral activity during recording, subsequently amplifying noise within the acquired EEG data. In certain instances, the signal can be entirely corrupted, necessitating reacquisition. The quality of EEG signals is particularly impacted by noise stemming primarily from non-cerebral activities occurring at the time of recording.

    Non-cerebral activities can be classified into two primary categories. The first category encompasses physiological activities originating from organs other than the brain within the human body, such as muscles and limbs. The second category involves external environmental factors. The impact of an eye blink on the acquired EEG signal quality is illustrated in Figure 1.

    Numerous advancements in feature extraction and classification techniques have emerged in recent years, aimed at enhancing the performance of Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) applications. Nonetheless, the effectiveness of BCI algorithms is significantly influenced by the EEG data recording process. Traditionally, the approach involves visually inspecting the recorded signal and discarding highly corrupted segments that are evidently tainted by noise. Yet, certain inherent noise characteristics within the signal remain imperceptible. The ability to gauge the quality of recorded EEG signals is crucial to identify these attributes and emphasize only the reliable segments.

    The principal objective of this section is to devise an automated quality assessment metric for input EEG signals by generating automated scores that quantify signal quality during recording. These scores are established on biological and mathematical features that necessitate signal processing techniques. This concept bears multiple advantages, including:

    • Real-time quality assessment:
      During EEG signal recording, our system will promptly issue alerts if any channel exhibits abnormal behavior. This empowers researchers to decide whether to halt recording to address noise-related concerns or proceed with the recording process.
    • Significant role in BCI applications:
      The proposed scores can serve as input for BCI applications, enhancing their accuracy. Brain commands can be treated with varying degrees of confidence based on signal quality.

    Throughout the EEG acquisition procedure, electrodes capture signals originating from a specific number of neurons. The challenge arises from the limited control over the number of neurons captured, making any excessive spikes detrimental to the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). While it's impractical to record neural signals devoid of biological noise, detecting or removing such noise post-recording and analysis is feasible. Managing technical factors hinges on understanding the SNR magnitude. The intricacy in minimizing technical factors stems from the SNR level itself. To validate our scores' accuracy, we meticulously recorded a clean signal and subsequently introduced noise. The noise percentage was controlled based on the known SNR value of the original signal.

    Addressing the conundrum of reducing environmental noise necessitates a clear demarcation between signal and noise, while safeguarding signal quality during noise removal. Employing the wavelet transform method, as proposed previously, is one approach to noise reduction. However, this method assumes that signal magnitude consistently outweighs noise magnitude in any wavelet representation—a presumption not universally valid for neural signal recordings. This research's objective isn't solely noise reduction in EEG signals but primarily to appraise EEG signal quality.

    EEG Data Formats

    Electroencephalography (EEG) stands as a pivotal technology in the realm of neuroscience and neuroimaging, allowing the non-invasive monitoring of electrical activity within the human brain. To harness the immense potential of EEG data and derive meaningful insights, it is crucial to understand the various data formats that encapsulate this neural information. EEG data formats serve as standardized structures that facilitate the representation, storage, and sharing of EEG recordings and their associated metadata. These formats play an indispensable role in enabling seamless communication between EEG acquisition systems, analysis software, and researchers across the globe.

    As the field of neuroscience continues to evolve, EEG data formats play an instrumental role in ensuring interoperability, preservation, and collaboration among researchers and institutions. A nuanced understanding of these formats empowers scientists to efficiently harness the wealth of neural insights concealed within EEG recordings, ultimately advancing our understanding of cognition, behavior, and neurological disorders.

    .EDF Format

    The .EDF (European Data Format) file format is a widely used standard for storing and exchanging biomedical time-series data. Developed by the European Society for Sleep Research and the European Data Format Committee, this format is specifically designed to accommodate various types of physiological signals, such as electroencephalography (EEG), electrocardiography (ECG), and electromyography (EMG), among others.

    The .EDF file format is renowned for its flexibility and compatibility with different data acquisition systems and software applications. It stores multi-channel data in a structured manner, allowing for the representation of diverse physiological measurements collected simultaneously. Each data record consists of a header section that holds essential metadata, followed by the corresponding signal samples.

    Key features of the .EDF format include its simplicity, making it accessible for both data storage and transmission, and its ability to handle long-term recordings, crucial in medical and research contexts. Additionally, the format provides information about signal characteristics, such as sampling frequency, physical units, and calibration, enabling accurate interpretation of the data.

    To ensure interoperability and widespread usability, various software tools and libraries support the .EDF format, allowing researchers and clinicians to analyze and manipulate the recorded physiological data. The accompanying .EDF+ format extends the standard to include annotations, markers, and events, enhancing its utility in applications like sleep studies and event-related data analysis.

    In essence, the .EDF file format plays an integral role in the field of biomedical signal processing, enabling efficient data exchange, long-term monitoring, and accurate analysis of physiological signals across diverse medical and research domains.
